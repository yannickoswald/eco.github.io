Order-of-Magnitude Economics

An introductory module in quantitative ecological economics

Ecological economists pay close attention to scale: the physical size of material and energy flows, the number of people involved, and the resources mobilised by the monetary economy. A tonne versus a million tonnes, or a thousand transactions versus a trillion, is not just a quantitative distinction – it reshapes possibilities, pressures and planetary impacts.

In this chapter we practise the physicist’s habit of asking “What is the right order of magnitude?” before fine-grained modelling. You will meet basic scaling laws, dimensional thinking, and worked examples ranging from global carbon fluxes to city-level energy budgets.

1 What do we mean by “order of magnitude”?

Rule of thumb: moving one decimal place (×10) changes an order of magnitude. Tenfold jumps are therefore the natural yard-stick for comparing large and small.
Formal definition
Given a positive quantity \(Q\), write \(Q = a \times 10^{n}\) with \(1 \le a < 10\). Then the order of magnitude of \(Q\) is \(n\): \[ \operatorname{ord}(Q) \;=\; \left\lfloor \log_{10} Q \right\rfloor . \]

Examples:

  • \(150 \Rightarrow \operatorname{ord}=2\)
  • \(0.05 \Rightarrow \operatorname{ord}=-2\)
